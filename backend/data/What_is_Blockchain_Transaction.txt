Blockchain is a decentralized digital ledger that records transactions across a network of computers, ensuring transparency and security. The global blockchain market is projected to reach $163.83 billion by 2029, growing at a rate of 56.3%, highlighting its increasing adoption and potential. To understand what a blockchain transaction is, it's important to recognize why it is gaining popularity. Its relevance in industries like finance, supply chain, and healthcare makes it an in-demand technology for the future. What is Blockchain Transaction? Key Concepts and Examples Explained A blockchain transaction is the transfer of data or assets between parties that is recorded on a decentralized digital ledger. Blockchain transactions operate on a peer-to-peer network, where each transaction is confirmed by multiple participants, making it resistant to tampering. In the next step of this process, once a transaction occurs, it is grouped with others into a "block" and added to a chain of previous transactions. These transactions are then verified using consensus mechanisms like proof-of-work or proof-of-stake. Since consensus mechanisms need multiple participants (nodes) to confirm the legitimacy of a transaction, it can prevent malicious transactions. Let’s take an example and understand how blockchain transactions work. Example of Blockchain Transaction To understand the workings of blockchain transactions, let’s consider a Bitcoin transfer from one person to another using blockchain technology. Here’s a blockchain example showing transactions. A company in one country wants to send a payment to a supplier in another country. The sender initiates the transaction by creating a request with payment details (amount, recipient details, etc.), signed with their private key. The transaction is broadcast to a decentralized blockchain network, which includes nodes from multiple participants (banks, financial institutions, etc.) in different regions. The blockchain network validates the transaction through consensus mechanisms (such as proof-of-work or proof-of-stake), ensuring that the funds are legitimate and the sender has enough balance to complete the transfer. Once validated, the transaction is grouped into a block and added to the blockchain, completing the transfer from the sender to the recipient. The entire transaction history is recorded, ensuring that both parties can track the payment in real-time. Blockchain includes blocks ( containing transaction data), chains ( links blocks in chronological order), and consensus mechanisms (such as proof-of-work) that ensure data integrity. These components work together to perform transactions. Let’s understand the workings of blockchain technology in detail. Also Read: What is Blockchain Technology? [Explained] Fundamentals of Blockchain Technology: An Overview Blockchain technology includes components like hash encryption, authentication methods, and mining mechanisms, which work together to ensure secure and trustworthy transactions. Here’s an overview of the components involved in blockchain technology. Hash Encryptions A hash function takes input data (like a transaction) and gives a fixed-size output, known as the hash. Each blockchain block contains the hash of the previous block, creating a chain. This makes sure that once data is recorded, it cannot be modified. Example: In Bitcoin, the hash of each block includes the hash of the previous block, creating an unbreakable chain. Authentications and Authorizations Authentication in blockchain ensures that only authorized users can participate in transactions. Public and private keys are used for this purpose—public keys act as identifiers, while private keys are used to sign transactions. Authorization grants permission for users to perform certain actions, such as sending or receiving assets. This prevents unauthorized people from engaging in specific blockchain activities. Example: When Raj sends Bitcoin to Kumar, he signs the transaction with his private key. Only Kumar can use his private key to access and spend the Bitcoin. Also Read: Exploring Cryptography in Blockchain: Types and Key Algorithms Mining The blockchain mining process adds new blocks to the blockchain. Miners compete to solve complex cryptographic puzzles, and the first to solve gets the right to add a new block to the blockchain. Mining maintains the decentralized nature of the blockchain by ensuring that transactions are checked and recorded. This is why successful miners are rewarded with cryptocurrency. Example: In Bitcoin, the first miner to solve it validates the transaction and adds a new block to the blockchain, receiving a reward in the form of a Bitcoin. Proof of Work Proof of Work (PoW) is a consensus mechanism in which miners must solve a cryptographic puzzle to confirm transactions and add a new block to the chain. The difficulty of the puzzle keeps the system secure and prevents malicious actors from taking control. Example: In Bitcoin's PoW system, miners can add a block to the blockchain only after solving a difficult mathematical problem. Proof of Stake In this alternative method, validators are selected based on the number of tokens they possess and are willing to "stake" as collateral. The more tokens are staked, the higher the chance of getting chosen to validate the next block. PoW offers strong security but at the cost of high energy consumption and lower scalability, while PoS is more energy-efficient, faster, and scalable. Example: In Ethereum 2.0, validators are chosen based on how the amount of Ethereum staked. In addition to these, smart contracts, decentralized applications (dApps), and cryptocurrencies also form a part of blockchain technology.
